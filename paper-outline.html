ACIS 2026 Paper Outline

Working Document

Target Venue

Australasian Conference on Information Systems (ACIS) 2026

Warning

Deadline and location TBC. Adjust travel budget line if held in Perth.

Working Title

“Does a Conversational Nudge Close the AI Literacy Gap? A Controlled Study of Scaffolded AI Interaction in Higher Education Assessment”

Authors

Michael Borck, Marcela Moraes, Torsten Reiners [, additional co-I TBC]

1. Introduction

  • AI tools in HE assessment are creating a new form of structural disadvantage
  • The skills required to use AI effectively are not equally distributed
  • Passive delegation vs active conversation: not the same thing, not the same outcomes
  • The equity dimension: students with fewer informal support networks are more dependent on in-class scaffolding
  • Research questions:
    1. Does a minimal conversational nudge improve AI interaction quality and task outcomes?
    2. Is the effect uniform across student cohorts, or differential by SES and first-in-family status?

2. Literature Review

  • AI in higher education assessment (Kasneci et al., 2023; Stojanov et al., 2024; Mollick & Mollick, 2023)
  • The delegation problem: passive AI use may produce worse outcomes than no AI use
  • Scaffolding and ZPD (Vygotsky, 1978)
  • Conversational framework for learning (Laurillard, 2002)
  • Nudge theory applied to learning environments (Thaler & Sunstein, 2008)
  • Guided vs unguided LLM use (Frontiers in Psychology, 2025)
  • Equity and digital literacy gaps in higher education
  • See Literature Review for expanded treatment

3. Theoretical Framework

  • Conversation, Not Delegation (Borck, 2026) as the organising framework
  • Scaffolding theory grounding the nudge mechanism
  • Nudge theory grounding the interface design choice
  • The misinformation friction effect: the nudge surfaces errors before they are accepted

4. Method

  • Design: Randomised controlled lab sessions across 3+ units (IS, Marketing, Supply Chain)
  • Participants: ~60-120 students; equity cohort flags (low SES, first-in-family) via de-identified linkage
  • Intervention: 10-variant nudge pool appended to AI responses; randomly selected per turn
  • Measures:
    • Automated: turn count, word count, response latency
    • Human-coded: Follow-Up (FU), Challenge (CH), Extension (EX); delegation derived
    • Task completion: 1-5 rubric scored by co-investigators within unit
    • Exit survey: confidence calibration, self-reported interaction style, condition awareness
  • Analysis: Linear mixed-effects models (condition as fixed effect, unit as random intercept); both unweighted and challenge-weighted scoring; equity interaction terms
  • See Methodology for full detail

5. Results

Note

To be completed after data collection (July-August 2026).

Key results to report:

  • Nudge effect on primary measures (proportion_challenge, mean_turn_score)
  • Effect sizes (Cohen’s d, marginal R-squared)
  • Equity-disaggregated results (interaction terms)
  • Confidence calibration analysis (within-unit)
  • Condition awareness (Q8a) and mechanism interpretation
  • Unweighted vs weighted scoring comparison

6. Discussion

  • Interpretation against the Conversation, Not Delegation framework
  • Equity implications: uniform benefit vs differential benefit
  • Misinformation friction effect (if observed in transcripts)
  • Confidence calibration: do delegators overestimate their output quality?
  • Limitations:
    • Sample size and power
    • Single-session design (persistence unknown)
    • Lab setting vs naturalistic AI use
    • Hawthorne effect (students know they are observed)
  • Null result scenario: equally valuable, redirects toward more structural interventions

7. Implications for Practice

  • Scalable, low-cost intervention for any AI-integrated unit
  • Open-access resource pack (facilitation guide, nudge variants, task design, survey instrument)
  • Contribution to Curtin’s Assessment 2030 agenda
  • Evidence base for NBF equity reporting from 2027
