Exit Survey

Version 2.0, March 2026

The exit survey is administered at the end of each lab session via Qualtrics (Curtin institutional licence). Estimated completion time: 8-10 minutes.

Why the Exit Survey is Needed

The transcript data tells us what students did. The exit survey tells us three things transcripts cannot:

  1. Confidence calibration - Do students who delegated to the AI believe their output is good? Comparing self-reported confidence (Q2) against task completion scores tests whether passive delegation produces unwarranted confidence in incorrect outputs. This is central to the misinformation risk argument: the harm of delegation is not just weaker output, but weaker output the student believes is strong.

  2. Self-report vs observed behaviour gap - Do students know how they interacted? Comparing self-reported interaction style (Q5, Q6) against coded transcript behaviour reveals metacognitive awareness gaps. A student who reports “I challenged the AI throughout” but whose transcript shows predominantly delegation turns lacks awareness of their own AI interaction patterns. This gap is a finding in itself and an argument for structured scaffolding.

  3. Baseline covariates - Prior AI experience (Q9, Q10) and formal AI instruction (Q11) provide covariates for the primary analysis. If random assignment produces groups that differ on prior experience, these allow statistical adjustment.

  4. Condition awareness - Did nudge-group students notice the nudge (Q8a)? This distinguishes between automatic behavioural cueing and conscious reflection as the mechanism of action.

Without the survey, the study can show the nudge changed behaviour but cannot explain why or test whether the behavioural change was accompanied by appropriate confidence calibration.

Administration

  • Facilitator displays the Qualtrics link on the main screen at the end of the task
  • Students complete the survey independently and silently on the lab computer
  • Facilitator confirms all students have submitted before dismissing the session
  • RA downloads responses after each session and links to transcript data via session code

Analytic Notes

Confidence calibration analysis (within-unit only)

Q2 (confidence) is compared against the task completion score for the same student, within each unit separately. Task completion scores require discipline-specific judgement and are not comparable across units (a score of 4 in IS does not equal a score of 4 in Marketing). Within each unit: Spearman correlation between Q2 and task score; comparison of the confidence-performance gap between nudge and control groups; identification of the “overconfident delegator” pattern (high confidence + low task score + high proportion_delegation from transcript coding).

Self-reported versus observed interaction style

Q5 and Q6 (self-reported interaction style) are compared against the RA-coded behavioural dimensions for the same conversation. Divergences between self-report and observed behaviour are analytically interesting - a student who reports directing the conversation but whose transcript shows predominantly delegation turns may lack metacognitive awareness of their own AI interaction patterns.

Q4 (noticing errors) and the nudge effect

Q4 is the most direct self-report measure of whether the nudge is producing the intended friction. Nudge group students who report noticing something wrong and correcting it are exhibiting exactly the behaviour the intervention is designed to produce. This question should be analysed first when reviewing preliminary results.

Q8a (condition awareness) and mechanism interpretation

Q8a checks whether students in the nudge group consciously noticed the appended text. If nudged students show improved interaction behaviour without reporting awareness of the nudge, this supports an automatic behavioural cueing mechanism. If they noticed it and report it changed their behaviour, the mechanism is conscious reflection. Both are interesting; the distinction matters for generalisability and for designing future nudge variants.

Q10 as a baseline covariate

Q10 (prior AI approach) is included as a covariate in the mixed-effects model if nudge and control groups differ on this baseline measure. See coding_scheme.md Part 7 for the full model specification.
